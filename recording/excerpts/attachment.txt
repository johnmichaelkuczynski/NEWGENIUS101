Attachment Theory and Mental Illness

The basic tenet of Attachment Theory is that a person's emotional attachments influence the way in which he deals with problematic situations and, moreover, that the way in which he deals with problematic situations influences his relations to others.

The beginnings of Attachment Theory as a scientific discipline can be traced to Sigmund Freud, who explicitly stated on several occasions that a person's character-architecture as an adult is largely a consequence of his relations in early life to his parents. Stated as simply as possible, Freud's position was that a psychologically healthy adult is someone whose parents loved him but did not spoil him, it being the mother's job to provide love and the father's job to provide discipline. A mother's love leads to self-love, and a father's discipline leads to self-discipline, with a person's success as an adult depending on his having a healthy combination of self-love and self-discipline.

There are four basic attachment patterns: Secure, Anxious-ambivalent, Anxious-avoidant, and Disorganized. Inadequate early-life attachments give rise not only to maladaptive personality traits but to full-blown psychopathology. Anxious-ambivalent early-life attachments are dispositive of neurosis. Psychopaths are likely to have had attachments of the disorganized variety. Psychopaths do not benefit from psychotherapy and they cannot have meaningful relations with others. But neurotics and psychotics can benefit from therapy.
